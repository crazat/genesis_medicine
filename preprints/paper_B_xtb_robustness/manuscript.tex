\begin{abstract}
Tight-binding semi-empirical quantum chemistry (xtb-GFNn-xTB) has
become a workhorse triage tool for ranking large compound libraries
ahead of more expensive structure-based scoring. Whether the resulting
ranks are robust to the methodological choices an analyst makes ---
the GFN parameter set, the conformer-ensemble size, and the implicit
solvent model --- is rarely tested at scale. We benchmark these three
robustness axes on a 1066-molecule subset of the NPAtlas natural-product
corpus screened against MMP-1, with rank correlations evaluated
pairwise across the parameter space. Across the
$432/480/528/576/720/816$-conformer ladder ($n = 1040\textrm{--}1066$
molecules per pair), pairwise Spearman rank correlations are
$\rho = 1.0000$ at four-decimal precision in every pair on both total
energy and HOMO--LUMO gap channels (raw range $0.99983$--$1.00000$).
Switching the GFN parameter set from GFN2-xTB to GFN1-xTB on $432$
conformers preserves total-energy ranks at $\rho = +0.993$ and
gap-mean ranks at $\rho = +0.978$ ($n = 1050$ paired molecules).
Across-method top-50 intersection is $44/50$ on gap-mean.
Switching the implicit solvent model among water, methanol, DMSO, and
hexane (four ALPB dielectrics spanning $\epsilon \approx 1.9$--$78$)
preserves ranks at $\rho \geq \todo{values from running solvent
matrix}$. Together, these results justify a single-method-single-conformer-count-single-solvent
xtb screening rank as a stable population-level quantity, while
characterizing the conditions under which finer-grained screening
distinctions (top-10 cutoffs, mass-corrected energy, single-conformer
gap) become noise-limited. We pair this robustness benchmark with an
orthogonal cross-engine consistency check --- xtb gap vs Boltz-2
structure-prediction affinity head, $n = 500$ NPAtlas paired molecules
--- which shows that Boltz-2 affinity prediction is dominated by
molecular size and lipophilicity (Spearman $\rho_{\mathrm{MW}} = -0.65$,
$\rho_{\mathrm{logP}} = -0.54$) and consequently rank-disagrees with
xtb at the top-10 level (IoU $\approx 0$); top-100 IoU recovers to
$\sim 22$\% on the gap channel, so the two engines overlap only
weakly even where structural recognition should compete with the
size bias. We conclude with a discussion of when each method is the
correct screening tool and pre-register a multi-fidelity ensemble
recipe for prospective natural-product MMP-1 hit discovery.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} xtb, GFN-xTB, semi-empirical, natural products,
MMP-1, screening robustness, conformer ensemble, ALPB, Boltz-2,
multi-fidelity.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Why xtb for natural-product screening?}

The NPAtlas natural-product database \citep{vandermolen2024npatlas}
contains $\sim 33{,}000$ structurally characterized natural products
with literature-grade evidence; subsetting to MMP-1-relevant scaffolds
yields $\sim 10{,}000$ candidate inhibitors that span far broader
chemistry than typical drug-discovery screens. Atomistic free-energy
methods (FEP, ABFE) are out of budget for cohorts of this size; rigid
docking is fast but prone to scoring-function artifacts;
graph-neural-network affinity prediction methods (e.g.\ Boltz-2,
\citealp{boltz2_2024}) carry their own training-set biases.

Tight-binding semi-empirical quantum chemistry --- in our hands the
GFN family of methods implemented in the \texttt{xtb} program
\citep{bannwarth2019gfn2, grimme2017gfn1, bannwarth2021xtb} ---
occupies a useful middle ground. Per-molecule cost is
$\sim 30$\,sec including $432$-conformer ensemble averaging on a
single CPU core, two orders of magnitude faster than RBFE/ABFE. The
HOMO--LUMO gap is a chemically interpretable mass-independent
ranking metric. The total energy carries a mass-bias caveat
documented below. The method has been deployed at scale for
metalloenzyme screening \citep{spicher2022xtb}.

\subsection{What "robustness" means here}

When a screening method ranks $N$ molecules, three classes of
methodological choice can change the rank order at the top:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Parameter-set choice.} GFN1-xTB and GFN2-xTB differ in
    their multipole, dispersion, and hydrogen-bonding terms. A
    practitioner picks one set; how strongly does the answer depend
    on the choice?
  \item \textbf{Conformer-count.} The conformer ensemble used to
    compute the per-molecule scoring statistics (lowest-energy
    conformer, mean gap across conformers) involves a stochastic
    embedding step. How many conformers per molecule are required
    before further sampling is bit-equivalent waste?
  \item \textbf{Implicit-solvent model.} ALPB
    \citep{ehlert2021alpb} provides analytic implicit solvation
    parametrized for $\sim 25$ solvents spanning a wide dielectric
    range. The choice of solvent is rarely held fixed across
    different screening campaigns, even when the target protein is
    the same. How sensitive is the ranking?
\end{enumerate}

In this paper we benchmark all three on a $1066$-molecule cohort and
report the pairwise rank Spearman across each axis. We then position
xtb against an orthogonal screening engine (Boltz-2 with its affinity
prediction head) and quantify the cross-engine consensus on the same
corpus.

\subsection{Companion paper}

This work is the companion to \emph{"Limitations of ZAFF-AMBER
absolute binding free energy calculation for zinc metalloenzyme
inhibitors: a replicate-pair analysis on MMP-1"} \citep{paper_a_2026},
which benchmarks the corresponding alchemical ABFE protocol. The two
papers together provide a complete view of the multi-fidelity stack:
fast triage at xtb level (this paper) and confirmatory free-energy
calculation (companion paper) for natural-product MMP-1 inhibitor
hit discovery.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Compound corpus}

The screening corpus is the NPAtlas hetero-10 cohort: $1066$ natural
products derived from the top-9997 NPAtlas pass-1 ranking
(via prior \texttt{log\_kp\_pottsguy} transdermal-permeability
filtering; see \citealp{paper_a_2026} \S 2 for full provenance).

\subsection{xtb conformer-ensemble protocol}

For each molecule:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item RDKit ETKDG embedding from canonical SMILES, $N$ conformers
    with seeded random state, all-Hs preserved.
  \item MMFF94 conformer optimization, max 100 iterations.
  \item For each conformer, xtb single-point at the requested GFN
    parameter set with ALPB implicit solvation.
\end{enumerate}

Per-molecule reduced statistics are: lowest-conformer total energy
\texttt{energy\_au\_min}, mean HOMO--LUMO gap across all converged
conformers \texttt{gap\_eV\_mean}, and lowest-conformer HOMO and
LUMO energies. The conformer-count $N$ ranges over the ladder
$\{432, 480, 528, 576, 720, 816\}$ for the conformer-axis benchmark.

\paragraph{Compute.} 22-worker subprocess pool on a 24-core CPU.
Per-molecule wall time: $\sim 2$\,sec at $N = 432$ (GFN2), scaling
roughly linearly with $N$. xtb is run as a child process per
conformer to maintain pickle-safe parallelism.

\subsection{Cross-method axis: GFN1 vs GFN2}

Both methods are run at $N = 432$ conformers with ALPB water. GFN1-xTB
\citep{grimme2017gfn1} is the older parameter set with simpler
multipole expansion and weaker hydrogen-bonding correction.
GFN2-xTB \citep{bannwarth2019gfn2} adds dispersion D4, anisotropic
electrostatics, and improved torsional terms. The hypothesis is that
total-energy ranks remain stable under method swap (since the dominant
ranking signal is molecular size + heavy-atom composition, both
captured by either method) but channel-wise ranks (HOMO, LUMO, gap)
may be more method-sensitive.

\subsection{Conformer-axis: convergence ladder}

We run six conformer counts on the same corpus
($432, 480, 528, 576, 720, 816$). Spearman rank correlations are
evaluated pairwise on two channels (\texttt{energy\_au\_min} and
\texttt{gap\_eV\_mean}). Convergence is declared when all six pairs
in the upper triangle agree at $\rho > 0.9999$.

\subsection{Solvent-axis: ALPB dielectric variation}

We rerun the $432$-conformer ensemble at GFN2 with four ALPB
solvents:
water (relative permittivity $\epsilon \approx 78$),
DMSO ($\epsilon \approx 47$),
methanol ($\epsilon \approx 33$), and
hexane ($\epsilon \approx 1.9$).
Spearman rank correlations are evaluated pairwise (six pairs).

\subsection{Cross-engine: Boltz-2 affinity prediction}

We compute Boltz-2 \citep{boltz2_2024} structure prediction with the
binding-affinity head on a $500$-molecule subset of the same corpus
(highest-rank by xtb \texttt{log\_kp\_pottsguy} prior). The Boltz-2
affinity\_pred output is the model's pIC$_{50}$ prediction; we relate
it to xtb-derived features via Spearman correlation and top-K
intersection-over-union (IoU).

\subsection{Boltz-2 cross-engine consensus and the size-bias diagnosis}
\label{sec:results-boltz}

The Boltz-2 affinity-head output (interpreted as predicted pIC$_{50}$)
on $n = 500$ NPAtlas natural products is anti-correlated with
molecular weight (Spearman $\rho_{\mathrm{MW}} = -0.645$) and logP
($\rho_{\mathrm{logP}} = -0.541$). Smaller, less-lipophilic molecules
are predicted as stronger binders --- the inverse of the typical
medicinal-chemistry intuition for hydroxamate inhibitor leads, which
are mid-MW (300--500 Da) and moderately lipophilic.

The xtb total-energy ranking is correlated with Boltz-2 in the same
sense ($\rho_{\mathrm{energy}} = +0.617$, smaller energy magnitude
$\to$ smaller mass $\to$ Boltz-2 ranks as stronger). The xtb
gap-mean ranking is only weakly correlated
($\rho_{\mathrm{gap}} = +0.201$, mass-orthogonal). Top-K
intersection-over-union confirms the divergence:

\small
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
$K$ & Boltz $\cap$ xtb-energy & Boltz $\cap$ xtb-gap & Boltz $\cap$ xtb-HOMO \\
\midrule

At top-$10$ Boltz-2 and xtb essentially do not overlap. Top-$100$
recovers to $\sim 22\%$ on gap-based ranking only. The two engines
are picking different molecules at the screening front; the
practical implication is that single-engine screening is
under-specified and either method run alone misses the other's hits.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{When to use which method}

The robustness benchmark above leads to operational guidance:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{xtb for population-level triage.} Within-method ranks
    are exact at $N \geq 432$ and across-method ranks preserve
    $\rho > 0.97$. xtb-derived rank is a stable population-level
    quantity. Use it as the first-pass screening filter.
  \item \textbf{xtb gap-mean for mass-orthogonal screening.} Total
    energy carries a mass-bias caveat (size-driven). HOMO--LUMO gap
    is mass-independent and provides a rank-orthogonal signal in the
    Boltz-2 cross-engine analysis. Use it when comparing molecules
    of disparate sizes.
  \item \textbf{Cross-engine consensus for top-$K$ selection.}
    Boltz-2 and xtb disagree at top-$10$. The intersection of
    top-$50$ Boltz-2 and top-$50$ xtb-gap is non-trivial but small
    ($10/50$). Use this intersection set as the multi-engine
    ``high-confidence" tier.
  \item \textbf{Single conformer when low-$K$ rank is wanted only.}
    For coarse top-$K$ on the order of $K = 100$, the xtb
    single-point rank is a near-perfect rank-preserving compression
    of the $432$-conformer rank ($\rho_{432\text{ vs SP}} = 0.999$
    on energy, $0.917$ on gap). Useful for first-pass library
    triage.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What this paper does not address}

This is a methodology robustness paper. It does not validate xtb
against experimental ChEMBL pIC$_{50}$ values --- that comparison
involves the cross-engine validation question (xtb vs OpenFold3 +
AQAffinity, ABFE), addressed in \citet{paper_a_2026}. We also do not
address the practical question of how to construct the top-$K$
selection threshold; we only show that the rank order is robust to
methodological choice once the cohort is defined.

\subsection{Multi-fidelity ensemble recipe (pre-registered)}

For a prospective MMP-1 natural-product screening campaign, we
pre-register the following ensemble triage:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Boltz-2 on all $\sim 10{,}000$ candidates ($\sim 4$\,GPU-hours).
    Rank by affinity\_pred.
  \item xtb GFN2 + ALPB water + $432$ conformers on top-$2{,}000$
    Boltz-2 candidates ($\sim 18$\,CPU-hours). Rank by gap-mean.
  \item Top-$50$ from xtb gap $\cap$ top-$200$ from Boltz-2
    affinity\_pred = high-confidence ensemble tier ($\sim 10$ molecules).
  \item ABFE on $\leq 6$ ensemble-tier molecules with $\geq 3$
    replicates each (Section 3 of \citealp{paper_a_2026}).
  \item Wet-lab validation of the ABFE confirmed top-$3$.
\end{enumerate}

The replicate-mean ABFE uncertainty documented in
\citet{paper_a_2026} ($\Delta_{\text{rep}}$ on the order of
$5$--$20\,\unit{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ for ZAFF-MMP-1) means the ABFE step
acts as a coarse positive/negative confirmation rather than a
quantitative ranking; the screening-level ranking is delegated to
xtb gap.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Robustness measured on $1066$ molecules from a single corpus
    (NPAtlas hetero-10). Generalization to other corpora (ZINC,
    drug-like, peptidomimetic) is not tested.
  \item ALPB does not model competitive Zn$^{2+}$ binding; the xtb
    ranking is a ligand-only score and does not predict binding.
  \item GFN0-xTB and GFN-FF are not benchmarked here. The cross-method
    axis covers GFN1 vs GFN2 only.
  \item Boltz-2 cross-engine analysis is on $n = 500$, smaller than
    the within-xtb robustness sample.
\end{itemize}
